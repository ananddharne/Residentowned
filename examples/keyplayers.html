Key Players

A huge shift in this whole thing is that we’re not looking at individual homes that have been foreclosed, we’re not looking at individual people who have high needs or individual families, we’re not looking at individual veterans who are coming back and need a place to stay. We’re looking at these interrelationships and group dynamics.

Philanthropic Organisations

The IRS guidelines and regulations for foundations has very recently been clarified to allow an increase in PRI, traditionally, if a foundation was investing money in investments that were not the highest return, lowest risk investments, they could actually be sued because they were not being “financially responsible.” So that meant put your money in the most reliable wall street mutual funds as opposed to investing in a little bit higher risk lower return things that would have a much greater impact on your mission. So program related investment can significantly increase. As well as individuals being able to do social impact investing.

In this case we’d specifically be looking at a combination of a city land bank the would be buying up the foreclosed and boarded up properties but concentrating specifically on target neighbourhoods—anywhere from six to twelve blocks. So now you combine the multiple philanthropic organisations who are possibly consolidated through some entity, but I think it’s better to have them separate, going in and buying out rental properties, buying abandoned properties, buying out small apartment buildings, not hopefully making a whole lot of noise about it because if they do the people will want higher prices, but if you’ve got different buyers going in and beginning to buy these out and at the same time you may have the city or the county going win with a little bit more of a microscope to enforce code violations and make it clear that they’re going to be a little bit harder to do business with, so people might decide that they’ve had enough and are ready to sell.

There would need to be some philanthropic dollars that are going to support the training, but you have all these philanthropic organisations who are now owning the properties, so they want to train people who are going to be their tenants and eventually their mortgagees. And there may be some intermediary financial credit union that can play part of the role in doing this, but the idea would be that youve’’ got a friendly local philanthropic organzaition, maybe it’s a nonprofit real estate development company that gets established, or you work with the best partner in the philanthropic community that’s well-established, somebody like the Common Bond in minneapolis. And in the process of training the people, the vision is that their going to be putting in sweat equity in improving the property and improving the neighbourhood.

Working with Residents

At the same time you’d be working with residents, people who are owners to begin talking with them about plans to build a stronger neighbourhood. You might buy some of them out, but others you’d be helping to establish as part of the owners in the next generation.

Since the properties would be purchased at as a low a price as they could be, the resale price would be low and it would be a relatively low mortgage that they would be paying. And relatively low taxes because property values are so low. And we would work with the city and with the other organzations to make that as affordable as we could. But part of it is that the people are actively working on building up their neighbourhoods and remodelling their homes. Now, you then begin working with local contractors, locally owned businesses that are prioritised as the people to help do the plumbing and the electric, but you’re also training people to do the painting and all the labor stuff, so that people are putting sweat equity into the remodelling.

Training

Habitat for humanity also has training that they provide for people who are becoming the new owners and you don’t have to reinvent the wheel there, because there are a number of key things that you would need to do. You may be picking up a lot of abandoned properties, but the difference here is that you’re also picking up a lot of the rental properties from the slumlords in as many places as we can so we’ve got as high a percentage ability to transform this neighbourhood as practical. Once you’ve acquired the properties than you can be more public because you’re not as worried about the prices going up. And you can begin working with the renters to engage them in this potential to create a RON. In order to qualify, you’re not asking to come up with a down payment, they have to go through all the training.

Volunteers and Sponsors

You also build on the HH model of bringing in volunteers from churches, corporations, and others to come in and volunteer and help. We would use a software platform called “meet the need” this is a free platform that would be used as part of the tools for RON. This helps identify who’s doing what, who has what needs, how can things be done. You’re tapping into various people who have different tools and skills as they’re helping to build up the neighbourhood. But it’s not only for the neighbours, this reaches out to a lot of neighbouring churches, rotary clubs, other community org groups that are learning about this and finding out how their boy scout group, their girl scout group can come in and volunteer to help with neighbourhood cleanups or painting the fences or working with landscaping or taking the empty lot and so on. And we also can look for corporate or nonprofit sponsors to help with greenspace. SO maybe it is the “Jackson credit union” park, which is just a simple greenspace that was opened up by friendly vendors.

Veterans

So with the veterans, you would have vetern serving organisations and you may have some veterans that may have higher needs, but they’re woven in with veterans who may have lower needs but are more willing to serve. What you’re giving them is a purpose for neighbourhood stabilisation and safety. They’ve been patrolling the streets in Bagdad, they’ve been in Candir province in afganistan, now they’re making their presence visible, they’ve got their shirts, their hats… and there’s veteran serving organisations that are supporting them and they’re getting together twice a week to have their meetings on how to best achieve their mission in keeping the neighbourhood safe and walk friendly and what not. At the same time, they are establishing equity in homes that would give them stabization and value. So you’re giving them purpose, stabilisation and value. You can bring in national veteran service organisations and then you can bring in other groups to do some of the blight busting and things that need to be done.

Artists

The artist community could be another form of anchor communities… Springboard for the arts has toolkits to help make change happen. Community supported art is something we’d get going there. We would be leveraging the toolkits in these neighbourhoods and the surrounding neighbourhoods cause if you want to do community supported arts and you’re saying we have a bunch of artists moving into this twelve block area, and they wan to make a living. The idea is that you get direct payments to artists by people who want to buy their art in the community and they’re not having to go through middle men. And of course you can leverage this with all kinds of online things as well. Maybe you’ve got a mural or maybe you want to create something that’s very striking. Painted ladies in San Francisco.
